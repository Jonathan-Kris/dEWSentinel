01 — The problem

It looks healthy for months.
Then it collapses "overnight."

A sending domain reads green for 2–3 months, then deliverability craters. It wasn't overnight — reputation eroded the whole time, invisibly.

The sequencer dashboard stays green because it reports a lagging warmup score — fine right up until it isn't.

Reputation vs. what the dashboard shows"all green"
Decay is silent and continuous. The red line is the truth; the green line is all anyone sees.
02 — Why it's a structural trap

Three facts make late detection inevitable.

01 · The cliff is tiny
0.30%
~3 complaints per 1,000

Google wants bulk senders under 0.10% spam complaints and never at 0.30%. At cold-email volume, a handful of clicks crosses it.

02 · The metric hides itself
24–48h
Lagging & self-concealing

Postmaster updates late, and counts only inbox-delivered mail. Once filtering starts, complainers never see it — the reported rate can fall as reputation craters.

03 · The cost is asymmetric
days → weeks
Fast to break, slow to fix

Damage lands in days; recovery takes 4–8 weeks (Gmail wants 7 straight clean days). A miss costs weeks — a false alarm costs a day.

03 — The insight

Stop watching the complaint rate.

Watch the signals that move before it — and treat the call as risk management, not reporting.

The complaint rate is the last thing to break. By the time it spikes, the domain is already gone.

The earliest signal isn't Postmaster. It's the send itself.

The moat

The leading layer only exists because sending.ac owns the sending infrastructure. It already emits this telemetry — the same first-party stream warm.ac is built on.

A competitor layered on someone else's platform is blind to it.

dEWSentinel isn't a new pipeline — it's a predictive, customer-facing consumer of a stream sending.ac already produces.
